APPENDIX C: DEPEG DEDUCTIBLE COMPARISON
================================================================================

Per contract DepegShield.sol:
  USDC: 10% deductible (1000 bps) -> 90% payout -- BUT EXCLUDED FROM COVERAGE
  DAI:  12% deductible (1200 bps) -> 88% payout
  USDT: 15% deductible (1500 bps) -> 85% payout

Rationale for higher USDT deductible:
  USDT (Tether) has historically been considered higher risk due to:
  - Less transparent reserve attestation
  - Historical near-depeg events (2018, 2022)
  - Regulatory uncertainty
  The 15% deductible compensates for this elevated risk.

Rationale for USDC exclusion:
  USDC is the settlement token. If USDC depegs:
  - Vault assets (denominated in USDC) lose value simultaneously
  - Paying USDC claims during a USDC depeg creates a death spiral
  - The protocol cannot guarantee solvency if its own unit of account fails
  This is a critical and correct design decision.

NOTE ON IL FORMULA BEHAVIOR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  IL is symmetric in log-space: a +X% move produces the same IL as a
  corresponding -Y% move where (1+X)(1-Y)=1. Key reference points:

  Price change    r        IL%       Exceeds 2% deductible?
  -----------   ------   --------   -----------------------
  +/-10%        0.90/1.10  0.11%    No
  +/-20%        0.80/1.20  0.62%    No
  +/-30%        0.70/1.30  1.61%    No (close)
  +/-35%        0.65/1.35  2.15%    YES (barely)
  +/-40%        0.60/1.40  3.18%    YES
  +/-50%        0.50/1.50  5.72%    YES
  +/-60%        0.40/1.60  9.52%    YES
  +/-75%        0.25/1.75  18.35%   YES (capped at 11.7%)

  The 2% deductible effectively means ETH must move +/-35% or more in a
  single 30-day policy period to trigger any payout. This is a relatively
  rare event — roughly 3 out of 17 sampled months (18%).

  Loss ratio of 343% means the product pays out $3.43 for every $1 collected.

  However, several important caveats apply:

  1. SCALE MATTERS: With only 5 policies at $20K, the premium pool is tiny.
     At scale (e.g., 100 policies x $20K = $2M coverage), annual premiums
     would be $105,000, and a single Curve-type event would pay $1,800,000
     (90% of $2M). Loss ratio remains similar. The fundamental issue is that
     the covered protocol set includes very few exploitable targets.

  2. LOW FREQUENCY, HIGH SEVERITY: Only 1 trigger in 5 years across 5
     blue-chip protocols. The expected annual trigger rate is ~0.2 events/year
     for the covered set. But when it triggers, the binary 90% payout is
     large relative to premiums.

  3. DUAL TRIGGER PROVIDES REAL FILTERING: Of the 12 major exploits in
     2020-2025, only 1 affected a covered protocol AND met the dual trigger.
     The 25% gov token threshold filtered out many governance incidents that
     were not true exploits.

  4. COMPARISON TO TRADITIONAL INSURANCE: Cyber insurance loss ratios of
     60-80% are considered healthy. ExploitShield at 343% is deeply
     unprofitable. To reach a 70% loss ratio:

     Required annual premium = $90,000 / (5 years x 0.70) = $25,714/year
     Required pBase at current scale: 25,714 / (100,000 x 1.3125) = 19.6%

     This is far too expensive for the market. The product needs either:
     (a) Much larger policy pool to spread the risk
     (b) Lower coverage per policy
     (c) Higher deductible (e.g., 30% instead of 10%)
     (d) Reinsurance for tail events

  5. AAVE EXCLUSION IS CRITICAL: The code correctly excludes Aave (line 164)
     because the vault deposits funds into Aave. If Aave were covered and
     exploited, the vault would lose both its deposits AND owe payouts —
     a catastrophic correlation. This is sound risk management.


RECOMMENDATIONS FOR EXPLOIT SHIELD PRICING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Option A: Raise pBase to 1200 bps (12%) — agressive but may deter buyers.
    5-year premiums: $78,750. Loss ratio: 114%. Still unprofitable.

  Option B: Raise pBase to 2000 bps (20%) — near-prohibitive.
    5-year premiums: $131,250. Loss ratio: 68.6%. Barely sustainable.

  Option C: Keep pBase at 400 bps but REQUIRE larger pool size.
    At 50 policies x $50K = $2.5M coverage:
    Annual premium = 50 x $50K x 4% x 1.3125 = $131,250
    5-year premiums = $656,250
    Max payout per event = $2.5M x 90% = $2,250,000
    Loss ratio if 1 event: 342% — same problem at scale.

  Option D (RECOMMENDED): Tiered pricing by protocol risk score.
    - Tier 1 (Compound, MakerDAO, Uniswap): 300 bps — battle-tested, never exploited
    - Tier 2 (Curve, Morpho): 800 bps — Curve was exploited, Morpho is newer
    The contract already supports tiers via protocolTier (uint8) in ExploitData.
    Combined with a reinsurance layer for catastrophic events, this could
    make the product viable.
